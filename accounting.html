The Threats Accounting Firms Face

Accounting practices hold sensitive financial information making them attractive targets:

Business Email Compromise

Attackers impersonate accounting firms or clients to redirect tax refunds, GST payments, or other financial transactions. BEC is among the top threats to Australian businesses per ASD.

Tax Fraud & Identity Theft

Client tax file numbers, financial records, and personal information enable identity fraud. According to ASD, identity fraud is the most commonly reported cybercrime.

Ransomware

Practice management systems, client files, and tax return data are critical to operations. Ransomware attacks (11% of incidents per ASD) can shut down practices during busy season.

Phishing Attacks

Staff receive fraudulent emails appearing to come from ATO, clients, or software vendors. Phishing was the initial access method in 38% of incidents reported to ASD.

Supply Chain Risks

Accountants integrate with multiple platforms—practice management, tax software, MYOB, Xero, Class Super, BGL—each representing potential attack vectors.

Insider Threats

Staff with access to all client files, former employees retaining system access, or inadequate access controls create risk of intentional or accidental data compromise.

Common Security Gaps in Accounting Practices

Based on security assessments across accounting practices:

Insufficient Access Controls

  • Weak passwords across practice management systems
  • No multi-factor authentication on critical platforms
  • Staff access to all client files regardless of need
  • Former staff retaining system access

Email Vulnerabilities

  • Missing email authentication (enabling firm impersonation)
  • No advanced phishing protection
  • Inconsistent staff training on recognizing fraud attempts

Backup Deficiencies

  • Backups not regularly tested
  • Backup systems accessible to ransomware
  • Local-only backups without offsite redundancy
  • No documented recovery procedures

Remote Access Risks

  • Staff accessing systems from home without VPN
  • Personal devices used for work without management
  • Weak remote access controls

Third-Party Platform Security

  • Multiple software vendors with varying security
  • Limited visibility into vendor practices
  • No systematic vendor assessment

Incident Response Gaps

  • No documented breach procedures
  • Unclear data breach notification requirements
  • Limited incident detection capabilities

Professional & Regulatory Obligations

Accounting firms face multiple security-related obligations:

Tax Practitioners Board (TPB) Requirements

Tax practitioners have obligations to protect client information under the Tax Agent Services Act and TPB Code of Conduct.

Privacy Legislation

Accounting firms handling personal information must comply with Australian Privacy Principles. Security controls support privacy obligations.

Professional Indemnity Insurance

PI insurers increasingly require evidence of security controls and regular assessments. Premiums have increased significantly for firms without documented security programs.

Professional Standards

Accounting bodies expect members to protect client information appropriately. Security frameworks support professional standards.

Frequently Asked Questions
